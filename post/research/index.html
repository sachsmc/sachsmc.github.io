Organizing your approach to a research study

Organizing your approach to a research study

Before discussing with statistician

  1. State the overall goal of the study

  2. State the specific scientific aims and how they relate to the overall goal

  1. Current state of scientific knowledge

  2. Identify the expected outcomes that the study is designed to address

  1. Consider the available data and its relationship to the aims

In consultation with statistician

  1. Refine scientific aims into statistical questions
  1. Identify the type of question
    1. Description/quantifying distributions, e.g., what is the distribution of X in the population?
    2. Comparing distributions/estimating associations, e.g., what is the relationship between X and Y? Does the relationship between X and Y differ by Z?
    3. Prediction, e.g., can I predict future/unobserved values of Y using X?
    4. Clustering of observations
    5. Clustering of variables
  2. Where appropriate, specify statistical hypotheses in terms of a summary measure for the distribution
    1. E.g., mean, median, hazard ratio, proportion, event rate
  1. Consider the design of the ideal experiment
  1. Ignoring practical and ethical limitations, how would you design a study to address the scientific aims?
    1. Who would be the subjects
    2. What would be the intervention
    3. How would subjects be assigned to intervention
    4. What variables would be measured
  1. Available data
  1. Sampling sceme
    1. Retrospective vs prospective
    2. Observational vs interventional
    3. Inclusion and exclusion criteria
    4. How was the sample size determined
  2. Variables in the dataset
    1. Names and relationship to real world quantities
    2. Conditions under which they were measured
    3. Units of measurement
  3. Categorization of variables according to meaning
    1. Demographic
    2. Baseline physiology
    3. Baseline risk factors, prognosis
    4. Measures of treatment
    5. Measures of ancillary clinical course during follow up
    6. Clinically relevant outcome
  4. Categorization of variables according to use in the analysis
    1. Response/outcome
    2. Predictor of interest
    3. Subgroups of interest
    4. Potential confounders 1. Associated with response (in truth) 2. Associated with predictor of interest (in the sample) 3. Not in causal pathway of interest
    5. Precision variables 4. Predictive of response, but not associated with predictor of interest
    6. Surrogates for response 5. Variables in causal pathway (mediators) 6. Variables measuring a later effect of response
    7. Instruments for treatment 7. Variables that cause the treatment/predictor of interest 8. Not associated with outcome through any other route
    8. Other/irrelevant

Statistician on their own

  1. Statistical analysis plan
  1. Primary specific aim
    1. Outcome measurement
      1. Clinical definition
      2. Protocol definition
      3. Statistical summary of interest
    2. Predictor of interest
    3. Subgroups for effect modification 4. Contrasts of summary measure across subgroups
    4. Statistical hypotheses
      1. Differences (superiority or inferiority)
      2. Non-inferiority
      3. Approximate equivalence
    5. Statistical burden of proof
      1. Level of significance
      2. Descriptive criteria and precision
  2. Secondary and exploratory aims
    1. Supportive analyses using alternate outcomes
    2. Analyses restricted to particular subgroups
    3. Analyses considering specific mechanisms of action
    4. Exploratory analyses used for hypothesis generation
  3. Sensitivity analyses
    1. Analysis specifically designed to address assumptions used in primary analysis
    1. Description of assumptions that need to be addressed
    2. How can those assumptions be relaxed/assessed
  4. Describe statistical model
    1. Univariate estimation
    2. Two sample comparisons 10. Weighting/matching methods for reduction of confounding
    3. Regression methods
      1. Summary measures
      2. Link function
      3. Modeling of predictor of interest (dichotomized, continuous, transformed)
      4. Adjustment for covariates, and how they are modeled
      5. Modeling of interactions
      6. Regression parameters of interest
      7. Statistic used for estimation and testing
    4. Handling of missing data
  5. Tables and figures
    1. Description of sampling scheme actually attained 18. Timeframes and sample sizes 19. Frequency and timing of measurement 20. Missing data patterns
    2. Description of observations and baseline variables
    3. Description of outcomes
    4. Preliminary estimates and precision related to primary question
    5. Tables/figures for inference on primary and secondary questions
    6. Exploratory results
    7. Sensitivity analyses

Manuscript writing group: reporting results and interpretation

  1. Scientific background

  2. Statement of hypotheses

  3. Materials and methods

  1. Sampling scheme

  2. Basic description of sample

  1. Results
  1. Descriptive statistics
    1. Subjects and baseline variables
    2. Outcome measurements
  2. Results about primary question
    1. Estimates of effect 1. Point estimate 2. Interval estimates
    2. Decisions about hypotheses
      1. Binary decision
      2. Measure of confidence in decision
    3. Describe questions that arise during this analysis
  3. Results about pre-specified secondary questions
  4. Questions which demonstrate consistency or lack thereof across alternative approaches
  5. Results about questions that arose during this analysis and that the vast majority of readers agree could be answered by the data
  1. Discussion
  1. Elaboration on ways that these analyses address the overall goal
  2. Results of the data-driven and speculative analyses of the data

General approach to manuscript writing

  1. Start with the conclusions
  1. What you found and what you recommend
  1. What is the primary evidence for your conclusions?
  1. Key graphs and numbers that support your claims

  1. What methods and data did you used to obtain your findings?

  2. Literature review and introduction

  3. Go back to results and conclusions and give alternative explanations

  1. Why might you be wrong?
  2. What is the limit of applicability of your findings?
  3. What future research would be appropriate to follow up?

  1. Write the abstract using main points from a-e

  2. Give article to co-authors and ask them:

  1. What is the message?
  2. What evidence do we have for it?
  3. What questions arose during the reading that could/should be addressed?
